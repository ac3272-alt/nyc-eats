Cuisines

New York has strong representation across nearly every cuisine, but the experience of eating a particular type of food varies significantly depending on the neighborhood and the format of the restaurant. Understanding what a cuisine tends to offer in terms of setting, pace, and price helps narrow the field before looking at specific options. Some cuisines are better suited to quick meals while others are built around longer evenings where the pace of the meal is part of the point. Those differences are worth considering alongside the food itself when deciding where to go.

Korean

Korean food in New York spans a wide range of formats, from counter-service noodle shops to full-service barbecue restaurants where the meal is structured around grilling at the table. The cuisine tends to be served communally, with multiple dishes arriving together rather than in courses, which makes it well suited to groups. Solo dining works better at noodle and rice-based restaurants, which move at a faster pace. Flushing in Queens has the densest concentration of Korean restaurants in the city, covering everything from barbecue to regional soups and stews, though Manhattan's Koreatown along 32nd Street has a strong selection as well. Korean barbecue involves multiple rounds of meat grilled at the table, which means the meal moves at its own pace and works best when there is no time pressure on either end.

1. Bōm

Bōm is a Michelin-starred Korean tasting menu restaurant tucked inside a private room at the back of Oiji Mi in the Flatiron District. The name translates to spring in Korean and doubles as an abbreviation for Back of Oiji Mi. Chef Brian Kim leads the kitchen alongside chef de cuisine Justin Choung. The dining room holds around 18 people at a U-shaped counter with an open kitchen at the center and electric grills built into the counter seating.

The tasting menu runs around 12 courses and is priced at $275 per person. The meal begins with seafood-focused courses before moving into an extended sequence of grilled meats. The kitchen ages certain cuts under a layer of koji and nuruk, a pairing of Japanese and Korean fermentation starters, to build deeper flavor without drying the beef out. Past courses have included and seaweed, king crab with steamed egg in anchovy broth, black cod with braised radish and Meyer lemon, and a progression of beef cuts covering tenderloin, wagyu short rib, and dry-aged ribeye prepared in different ways. The meal closes with a rice and banchan course before moving into dessert. A wine pairing is available at $245 and leans toward smaller producers and less conventional wine regions.

The meal runs approximately two and a half hours across two seatings per evening.

Source: Bōm

2. Howoo

Howoo is a Korean barbecue restaurant on East 31st Street in Koreatown, opened in 2025 by the team behind Nubiani. The bi-level dining room spans 7,700 square feet with a dark, gold-accented interior, plush booths, and a bar where each seat has its own grill. The beef is dry-aged for at least 35 days on site, and servers manage the grilling rather than leaving it to diners.

The meal is structured around two set packages. The Prime package is $155 for two and includes four American wagyu cuts, banchan, a steamed egg soufflé, and a closing course of either kimchi stew or cold noodles. The Elite package at $195 for two adds A5 Miyazaki wagyu. The banchan rotates and has featured japchae, oyster mushrooms, kimchi, spinach, and lotus root. The tend to pair better with the rest of the meal than the kimchi stew. The meal finishes with green tea ice cream. A la carte ordering is available alongside the set packages.

Howoo sits within a larger complex that also includes DubuHaus, which focuses on handmade tofu, and Musaek, a cocktail bar. Reservations are strongly recommended. The restaurant is open Monday through Wednesday from 5pm to 11pm, Thursday and Friday from 5pm to midnight, and Saturday and Sunday from noon onward.

Source: Howoo

Italian

Italian food has a long history in New York, shaped by successive waves of Italian immigration that established the red sauce tradition across neighborhoods in Manhattan, Brooklyn, and the Bronx. That tradition still has a strong presence in the city, alongside a newer generation of restaurants doing more regionally specific Italian cooking, drawing from traditions in Emilia-Romagna, Sardinia, and other parts of the country. Pizza occupies its own category within Italian food in New York, with significant variation in style, technique, and quality across the five boroughs. The stronger Italian restaurants in the city tend to focus on a specific regional tradition or a limited set of dishes rather than covering the full breadth of Italian cuisine. Nolita, the West Village, and parts of Brooklyn have particularly dense concentrations of well-regarded Italian options across different price points and formats.

1. Torrisi Bar & Restaurant

Torrisi Bar & Restaurant is an Italian restaurant in the Puck Building on Mulberry Street in Nolita, operated by Major Food Group, the same group behind Carbone. It holds a Michelin star. The interior has soaring ceilings, an open kitchen, and a rear dining room with velvet booths. Servers wear cream jackets and the menus are printed like formal invitations. The building is a Nolita landmark and the dining room has an energy that is hard to place but easy to feel once you are inside.

The menu is technically Italian but draws from the neighborhoods around it, including Chinatown and the Lower East Side. The is one of the most ordered dishes, wrapping thin-sliced prosciutto around a warm fried dough. The fennel and grapefruit starter arranges shaved fennel over citrus, goat cheese, and Marcona almonds. Pasta options include a spaghetti with lamb amatriciana and a prawn-filled raviolini. There is always a rotating rotisserie item, which has included porchetta and lamb, both served with sauces on the side. The chicken, cooked over Japanese coals with a sauce made from the whole bird, has been a consistent highlight. Dessert is anchored by a sundae built around gelato, fudge, and coffee granita rather than a traditional affogato.

Reservations open 30 days ahead at 10am on Resy. A bar at the front of the restaurant holds 12 seats reserved exclusively for walk-ins of one or two people. Arriving before the 5pm opening gives the best shot at a seat. Lunch is considerably easier to book than dinner.

Source: The New York Times

2. Don Angie

Don Angie is an Italian-American restaurant on Greenwich Avenue in the West Village, opened in 2017 by husband-and-wife chefs Scott Tacinelli and Angie Rito. The dining room is compact with dark wood tables, leather seating, and a marble bar near the front. The space gets busy quickly and the atmosphere runs energetic most evenings.

The menu takes Italian-American cooking in a more inventive direction than most restaurants in that category. The is a sesame-crusted dough filled with cheese and butter, served as a full plate. The chrysanthemum salad arrives with a heavy layer of grated parmesan on top. The garganelli giganti is one of the more frequently ordered pasta dishes, built around guanciale, pecorino, and chunks of meatball in a ragu. The stracchino gnocchi with broccoli rabe and provolone is another option worth trying. The lasagna uses thin noodles and whipped robiola cheese in a San Marzano tomato sauce and is priced at $64. Entrees include grilled fish, a veal schnitzel finished with prosciutto and horseradish, and a grilled pork shoulder with gigante beans. The cocktail list has a cold brew and biscotti-infused vodka drink that functions as the house take on an espresso martini.

Reservations open seven days out at 9am on Resy. The bar at the front takes walk-ins. Dinner starts at 5pm Monday through Thursday and 4pm on Fridays and Saturdays. Weekend lunch runs from 11:30am with limited walk-in availability.

Source: Don Angie

Mexican

Mexican food in New York has expanded significantly over the past decade, with a growing number of restaurants drawing from specific regional traditions rather than presenting a generalized version of the cuisine. Restaurants that specialize in a particular style, whether Mexico City street food, Oaxacan cooking, or coastal mariscos, tend to produce more consistent and higher quality results than those covering a broader range. The format across most Mexican restaurants in the city is casual, with counter service and walk-in dining more common than reservations. At the more established spots, popular items can sell out before the end of service, so arriving earlier in the evening gives access to the full menu. Greenpoint in Brooklyn and parts of Jackson Heights in Queens have some of the strongest concentrations of serious Mexican options in the city.

1. Quique Crudo

Quique Crudo is a Mexican seafood restaurant on Bedford Street in the West Village from chef Cosme Aguilar, who also operates Casa Enrique in Long Island City, the first Mexican restaurant in the city to receive a Michelin star. The New York Times awarded it a Critic's Pick with one star in 2026. The space is one of the smaller restaurants in the neighborhood, with a black granite bar running through the middle and around 20 bar stools. Sitting at the bar gives a direct view into the open kitchen where most of the work happens. No reservations are taken.

The menu centers on ceviches, aguachiles, and tostadas, with most dishes in the mid-twenty dollar range. The is served on a coconut milk base with salsa macha. The scallop aguachile draws its depth from a combination of soy, Worcestershire, and Maggi. The crab tostada piles fresh lump crab over avocado crema and serrano on a thick masa shell. The fluke ceviche is a simpler preparation with lime, avocado, and red onion. One of the few dishes without seafood is the enmoladas, made with a mole recipe carried over from Casa Enrique. The cocktail list covers over 25 drinks with an emphasis on agave spirits.

The restaurant is open Monday through Saturday for dinner. Weekend evenings draw a crowd, and arriving earlier tends to mean a shorter wait for a seat.

Source: Eater NY

2. Taqueria Ramirez

Taqueria Ramirez is a taco counter on Franklin Street in Greenpoint, Brooklyn, run by chef Giovanni Cervantes and Tania Apolinar. The restaurant is modeled after Mexico City-style taquerias, using a choricera, a stainless steel vat common in Mexico City kitchens, to slow cook the meats in lard and spices for several hours before service. The space holds around 10 people, with counter seating facing the open kitchen. Most people eat outside. The restaurant does not offer takeout or delivery, and everything is served on plates that go back to the kitchen when finished.

The menu has six tacos, all priced around $4 to $5. The is a lower belly cut of beef stewed for around three hours until it breaks down completely. The longaniza is a house-made pork sausage with paprika and orange-hued juices. The campechano combines both. The tripa is blowtorched to order before being handed across the counter. The al pastor is cooked on a trompo rather than in the choricera and comes with a strip of pineapple. The nopales taco is cactus cooked with tomato, fava beans, and queso añejo. The condiment bar has red and green salsa, chopped raw onion, cilantro, and lime. Popular items like the suadero and tripa tend to sell out by 8pm or earlier, so arriving before peak hours gives more options.

The restaurant is open Tuesday through Sunday. The G train to Greenpoint Avenue is the closest subway stop, around a five minute walk away.

Source: The Infatuation

Thai

Thai food in New York covers a wide spectrum, from quick neighborhood restaurants serving standard pad thai and curries to more specialized spots drawing from specific regional traditions across northern, central, and southern Thailand. The cuisine accommodates both solo meals and larger shared tables depending on the number of dishes ordered, and the format is flexible enough to work across different occasions and budgets. Quality varies considerably across the city, and some of the stronger Thai restaurants are located outside of Manhattan, in neighborhoods like Carroll Gardens, Long Island City, and parts of Queens, where the focus stays on the cooking rather than volume. The East Village has a concentration of well-regarded Thai options as well, with several restaurants that go beyond the standard menu toward more regionally specific preparations.

1. Bangkok Supper Club

Bangkok Supper Club is a Thai restaurant on Hudson Street in the West Village from the same team behind Fish Cheeks in NoHo, led by chef Max Wittawat. The restaurant draws from Bangkok's late night dining culture, with deep U-shaped booths, an open kitchen, and a cocktail program that runs alongside the food. The space stays busy most nights and works well for groups.

The menu is strongest in the smaller plates. The places sashimi-grade scallops over pickled watermelon rind and diced watermelon with a chili watermelon granita on top. The fried duck egg comes topped with grated cured egg yolk and trout roe. The beef tongue is brined for over 24 hours in galangal and lemongrass before being charcoal grilled and served with tamarind sauce and toasted rice powder. A crab tartlet topped with uni and caviar is another standout from the starters. Among the larger plates, the crispy whole branzino is the most consistent option. The beef cheeks massaman curry and the pork jowl with fried rice are also on the menu. The cocktail list includes drinks made with fish sauce, truffle oil, and Thai spirits alongside more straightforward options.

The restaurant takes reservations. It works best as a spot for sharing several smaller dishes rather than committing to the larger entrees, which have received more mixed feedback than the starters.

Source: Resy Blog

2. Soothr LIC

Soothr LIC is the second location of the East Village Thai restaurant Soothr, opened in Long Island City in 2025. The original East Village spot received a New York Times Critic's Pick for its noodle-focused menu drawn from several regions of Thailand. The LIC location is considerably larger, with interiors referencing Bangkok's Yaowarat Chinatown district, a bar surface inlaid with mahjong tiles, neon lighting, and a chandelier in the front room. The space handles larger parties comfortably and has no time limit on tables, unlike the original location.

The full noodle menu from the East Village carries over, including the ba mii pu with egg noodles, crab, and roast pork, and the khao soi. The LIC location adds around 15 dishes not available at the original. The is one of the stronger additions, with whole crab in a yellow curry. The roasted duck from the Yaowarat section of the menu comes with a sesame and fish sauce dipping sauce. Large tiger prawns with a sticky glaze and potato starch noodles with crab in a light sweet sauce round out some of the shareable options. Dessert is presented as a mystery box that may contain mango sticky rice ice cream or mochi. The cocktail list includes a version of a Long Island iced tea that has been clarified and comes with a small jellied piece shaped like a mahjong tile.

The restaurant takes reservations and is open Monday through Friday from 11:30am and Saturday and Sunday from 11am, with a break between lunch and dinner service each day.

Source: Soothr LIC

Bluefin Tuna with Foie Gras

What makes it special

Bluefin tuna is paired with Hudson Valley foie gras and gamtae seaweed and served as a small taco. The tuna is fatty and clean, the foie gras adds richness, and the seaweed shell brings a grassy, savory finish seasoned with soy.

Spicy Cold Noodles

What makes it special

The spicy cold noodles are loaded with hot mustard and vinegar, which cuts through the beef fat from the barbecue and resets the palate between rounds of meat. A strong order to close the meal.

Ham Platter with Zeppole

What makes it special

Two types of ham, an American and an Italian, are sliced thin and served alongside golden zeppole dusted with black pepper and fried rosemary. A pineapple mostarda ties the two together into a combination that works better than it sounds.

Stuffed Garlic Flatbread

What makes it special

A thin, crispy focaccia-style bread stuffed with creamy stracchino cheese and garlic, topped with sesame seeds and garlic chives. Inspired by the Northern Italian focaccia di Recco. Most tables order a second.

Lobster Ceviche

What makes it special

Maine lobster ceviche served in a coconut milk base with ginger and topped with a nutty, spicy salsa macha. The coconut brings sweetness and the salsa macha adds heat and depth against the clean lobster.

Suadero

What makes it special

Suadero comes from the muscle between the ribs, sometimes called rose meat. At Taqueria Ramirez it is slow-stewed in a vat of broth and lard alongside the other meats until it breaks down into something rich, tender, and deeply savory.

Hokkaido Scallop Ceviche

What makes it special

Sashimi-grade Hokkaido scallops served over pickled watermelon rind and diced watermelon with mint and garlic, topped with a chili watermelon granita. The granita melts into the dish and keeps everything cold and bright against the rich scallop.

Dungeness Crab Karee

What makes it special

A whole Dungeness crab served in a velvety yellow karee curry. The shell runs bright red and the crab inside is tender and pulls apart easily. Best eaten with your hands. One of the strongest dishes added at the LIC location.
