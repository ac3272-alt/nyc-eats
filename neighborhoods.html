Neighborhoods

Your location in New York affects which restaurants make sense to consider. Some neighborhoods have enough options close together to choose on the spot, while others are better approached with a specific place in mind. Understanding how an area is laid out makes the search easier before you begin.

The Flatiron District sits in downtown Manhattan between 20th and 26th Streets, close to both Union Square and Midtown. The neighborhood takes its name from the Flatiron Building, a triangular structure completed in 1902 at the intersection of Fifth Avenue and Broadway. Madison Square Park sits on the northern end of the district. The area is primarily commercial and the dining options lean toward sit-down meals over quick stops.

Source: Time Out

Casa Mono

Casa Mono is a Spanish tapas restaurant on Irving Place near Union Square that has held a Michelin star since 2009. It takes reservations and seats around 13 tables. The menu focuses on Spanish coastal cooking with a range of small plates. It suits a longer meal where the goal is to stay in one place and eat across several courses.

The menu is built around small plates and covers a wide range of Spanish coastal cooking. Standout dishes include razor clams cooked on a plancha, oxtail-stuffed piquillo peppers, skirt steak with romesco, and octopus with fennel and grapefruit. Sitting at the bar directly in front of the grill gives a view of the plancha cooking. The wine list covers nearly 600 Spanish wines and has been recognized by Wine Enthusiast and Wine Spectator.

Casa Mono takes reservations and is open daily from noon to midnight. The bar next door, Bar Jamón, is a walk-in only Iberico ham and wine bar that shares the same wine list.

Source: The Infatuation

Coqodaq

Coqodaq is a Korean fried chicken restaurant in the Flatiron District opened by the team behind Cote Korean Steakhouse. It received a Michelin Bib Gourmand and appeared in the documentary series Knife Edge: Chasing Michelin Stars. Walking in, there is a handwashing station near the entrance, which makes practical sense given that most of the meal is eaten without utensils.

The primary way to order is through the Bucket List, a tasting format that starts with a chicken consommé and moves into a bucket of fried chicken across three cuts: nuggets, boneless thighs, and drumsticks. A second round of chicken can be ordered with either a soy garlic or gochujang glaze. Four sauces come alongside: Jun Verde, Honey Mustard, Pepper Parmesan, and Gochujang BBQ. Banchan and a scallion salad are included as well.

Reservations become available two weeks out at 10am and fill up fast. Tables for two are only bookable at the first or last slots of the day. The dining room runs loud and the tables are closely spaced, which is worth keeping in mind before going.

Source: Resy Blog

Rezdôra

Rezdôra is an Italian restaurant on East 20th Street in the Flatiron District that opened in 2019. The chef has Sardinian heritage and trained at Osteria Francescana in Modena, and the menu draws from the cooking traditions of Emilia-Romagna in northern Italy. The space is narrow with exposed brick, a walk-in bar along the front, and table seating toward the back.

The kitchen focuses heavily on handmade pasta, with around nine varieties on the menu at a time. Each bowl runs around thirty dollars and the portions are on the smaller side, making it common to order more than one. The cappelletti filled with leeks and the maccheroni al pettine with duck ragu are two of the more well-known dishes. The gnocco fritto, a fried dough served with cured meats, is worth ordering as a starter. The rest of the menu beyond pasta has received more mixed feedback.

The restaurant takes reservations for table seating. The bar is available for walk-ins. The dining room fills up and gets loud, and the compact layout means it works better for smaller parties.

Source: The New York Times

The East Village sits east of Broadway between Houston and 14th Streets. The neighborhood has a long history as a center for artists, musicians, and writers dating back to the 1960s, when it began to develop its own identity separate from the Lower East Side to its south. That history has carried over into one of the more varied dining scenes in the city, with options spanning cuisines, price points, and formats across a walkable stretch of blocks. The concentration of restaurants is dense enough that arriving without a reservation is workable most nights. The 6 train to Astor Place and the L train to First Avenue are the most direct subway options.

Source: NYC Tourism

Banh Anh Em

Bánh Anh Em is a Vietnamese restaurant on Third Avenue in the East Village from the same team behind Bánh uptown. The menu covers a wide range of Vietnamese dishes with the bánh mì as the main draw. The bread is made in house and goes through a steam oven process that produces a crisp outer crust with a soft interior. The OG bánh mì is built around three house-made cold cuts with pickled vegetables and herbs.

The menu extends well beyond sandwiches. The phở uses noodles rolled fresh daily and a beef broth seasoned with warm spices. The bún riêu is a soup built around crab meatballs, river snails, and crispy pork skin. The chả cá lã vọng is a marinated catfish dish finished at the table over a bed of dill and scallions. The bánh ướt chồng is an interactive rice roll format where diners fill and roll their own portions from a selection of meats and condiments brought to the table.

The restaurant does not take reservations. Dinner runs Tuesday through Sunday. Lunch is available on Fridays, Saturdays, and Sundays. Takeout is an option as well. Weekend evenings tend to draw long waits, so arriving early or going on a weeknight is the more practical approach.

Source: The New York Times

Mokyo

Mokyo is a Korean small plates restaurant on Saint Marks Place in the East Village from the team behind Thursday Kitchen. The menu is built around sharing, with dishes that blend Korean cooking with unexpected ingredient combinations. The wagyu oxtail spring roll is paired with gochujang and onion puree. The corn dumplings come with truffle salsa verde, fennel, and parmesan. A New York strip steak is served with a Korean mustard sesame preparation and crispy potato. Most dishes stay under fifteen dollars.

The interior has an industrial character with worn walls and exposed pipes. The space is small and fills up quickly most nights. The drink menu includes a signature light-up cocktail carried over from Thursday Kitchen. The sharing format works well when the goal is to try a range of dishes across the table rather than ordering individually.

Source: The New York Times

Okiboru House of Udon

Okiboru House of Udon is a Japanese noodle restaurant on Second Avenue in the East Village from the same team behind Okiboru House of Tsukemen on the Lower East Side. The kitchen specializes in himokawa-style udon, a noodle that runs significantly wider and flatter than the standard variety, made in house daily. The main format is a dipping set where the noodles come warm alongside a yuzu broth and a plate of tempura. The tempura selection includes bacon, shrimp, purple sweet potato, asparagus, and onion. A cold matcha udon is also on the menu and works better during warmer months. Bamboo tongs come with the meal to help handle the width of the noodles.

The dining room holds around 20 people and orders are placed through a QR code at the table. The pace of the meal is fast and most visits wrap up in under half an hour. There are no reservations and a line tends to form around opening each evening. Going on a weekday or during off-peak hours cuts the wait down considerably.

Source: The New Yorker

The Lower East Side sits south of the East Village, bounded by Houston Street to the north, the Bowery to the west, and Canal Street to the south. The neighborhood saw waves of immigration throughout the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, and that history runs directly through the food available here. The Jewish deli and appetizing shop traditions that defined the area for much of the last century still have a presence, and the restaurants that remain from that era are among the most established in the city. Beyond those older institutions, the blocks around Orchard and Delancey Streets have a wider range of restaurants covering different cuisines and price points. The F and M trains stop at Delancey Street and the J and Z trains stop at Essex Street.

Source: The Agency Journal

Wayla

Wayla is a Thai restaurant on Forsyth Street on the Lower East Side, set below street level in a basement space reached by a staircase. Inside there are two narrow dining rooms and a bar. A garden patio in the back is the main reason to go in warmer months and tends to book up faster than the interior.

The menu includes some dishes that are harder to find elsewhere in the city. The moo sarong are pork meatballs hand-wrapped in noodle and fried, a preparation that comes from Thailand and rarely appears on menus in New York. The branzino is deboned and fried in the kitchen before being plated with fresh herbs and shallots. The lobster noodle dish uses rice noodles and comes with a full lobster, priced at thirty-six dollars. The wok-fried string beans with tofu and the house-made pork sausage are simpler options that hold up well alongside the more involved dishes.

The restaurant takes reservations and seats walk-ins when space is available. Weekend evenings fill up quickly and a wait at the bar is common without a booking.

Source: Eater NY

Sami & Susu

Sami & Susu is a Mediterranean restaurant on Orchard Street in the Lower East Side from co-owners Jordan Anderson and Amir Nathan, who first worked together at Maison Premiere. The restaurant began as a pandemic-era pop-up in Williamsburg before moving to its current location, which opened in August 2021. It holds a Michelin Bib Gourmand designation.

The space is small, seating around 18 people, with an open kitchen visible from the dining room. During the day it operates as a pita shop, with sandwiches and rice bowls making up most of the menu. The chicken schnitzel pita with zhoug and the lamb over rice are among the regular lunch options. Dinner shifts to a broader Mediterranean-inspired menu of small and large plates served alongside natural wine. Dishes have included tuna crudo, lamb kebabs with malawach and sumac onion, whole branzino with leeks, and housemade pita paired with dips. The menu changes with the season.

Lunch runs from 11am to 4pm and dinner from 5:30pm to 10pm daily, with service until 11pm on Fridays and Saturdays.

Source: The Infatuation

Potluck Club

Potluck Club is a Cantonese-American restaurant on Chrystie Street on the Lower East Side from the team behind Milk & Cream Cereal Bar. The space sits near the border of Chinatown and Nolita. Inside there is a large dining room with murals and a bar toward the back. A counter near the kitchen takes walk-ins, and the layout handles larger groups well.

The dinner menu runs around a dozen dishes. The salt and pepper fried chicken is served with scallion biscuits and a chili plum jam on the side. The braised short ribs come with kabocha squash and are based on a preparation from head chef Zhan Chen's father. Other dishes include a jellyfish salad with Chinese celery and sesame, oyster mushroom rice roll noodles, Berkshire pork and chive potstickers with house-made chili crisp, and XO fried rice with shrimp and sausage. Dessert is a pineapple soft serve topped with pineapple bun crumble.

The restaurant is open Thursday through Sunday from 5:30pm to 10pm. Parties of eight or more book through email rather than the standard reservation system.

Source: Welcome to Chinatown

Williamsburg is in North Brooklyn, reachable by the L train from Manhattan to Bedford Avenue. The J, M, and G trains also serve the area. The neighborhood shifted from a manufacturing and working class community into an arts center starting in the late 1970s, when artists began occupying former warehouse spaces. Over the following decades it grew into one of the more established dining areas in Brooklyn, with restaurants spread across a walkable area between the East River waterfront and the neighborhoods to the south and east.

The dining options cover a wide range of cuisines and price points. Peter Luger Steakhouse on Broadway has been in the neighborhood since 1887. Lilia on Union Avenue draws reservations weeks in advance for its pasta. Maison Premiere on Bedford Avenue is known for its oysters and extensive cocktail list. Smorgasburg runs every Saturday at Marsha P. Johnson State Park from April through November, with dozens of local food vendors set up along the waterfront. Domino Park, built on the site of a former sugar refinery and opened in 2018, and McCarren Park to the north both give the neighborhood space to spend time beyond just the meal.

Source: NYC Tourism

The Four Horsemen

The Four Horsemen is a Michelin-starred wine bar and restaurant on Grand Street in Williamsburg, opened in 2015 and partly owned by James Murphy of LCD Soundsystem. The restaurant established itself around low-intervention wine before natural wine became as prevalent in the city as it is now. The list has since grown to over 800 bottles covering both natural and conventional producers, and the staff is knowledgeable and available to help guests navigate it.

The dining room is small, with cedar ceilings and a bar facing the kitchen. The menu changes each week and does not follow a fixed format. Past dishes have included butter beans in a parmesan rind broth, sweetbread skewers with egg yolk, simply dressed clams, and rotating pasta options. The kitchen focuses on straightforward preparations rather than elaborate ones, with the wine treated as an equal part of the meal.

Reservations open online 30 days in advance at 7am and tend to go quickly. Walk-ins are seated from 5:30pm, and arriving near opening is the most dependable way to get a table without a prior booking. The adjacent bar, Nightmoves, is available for those waiting for a table.

Source: Eater NY

The Residence of Mr. Moto

The Residence of Mr. Moto is a Japanese restaurant on Grand Street in Williamsburg from chef Tomotsugo Kubo, the same team behind The Office of Mr. Moto in the East Village and TabeTomo. The restaurant specializes in kaisen-don, a raw seafood rice bowl that is common in Japan but rarely the focus of a dedicated restaurant in New York. The interior is designed around a fictional character, with antique artwork, shelves of collected objects, and a bathroom behind a sliding bookshelf. The dining room seats 52 people.

The menu offers three kaisen-don options. The signature bowl comes with tuna tartare, scallops, surf clam, and yuzu tobiko. A milder version uses amberjack and sea bream sashimi with pickled daikon. A spicier version combines salmon and tuna tartare with yuzu tobiko. Each bowl is served with steamed rice, soy sauce, pickles, and a raw egg. When the rice is nearly finished, guests press a bell at the table and a server pours a 60-hour seafood broth over the remaining rice, a preparation called ochazuke. The broth carries over from the previous day. Beyond the kaisen-don, the menu includes sushi sets, sashimi, seared scallops, and desserts made with Brooklyn bakery Kitsby, including a matcha tiramisu and hojicha panna cotta. The drink list focuses on sake alongside low-ABV cocktails.

Reservations open 14 days in advance at 10am. Counter seats are available for walk-ins daily. The restaurant is open Tuesday through Sunday from 6pm to 11pm.

Source: Resy Blog

Antidote

Antidote is a Sichuan restaurant on South 2nd Street in Williamsburg, near Domino Park. The space is large with a skylight, exposed concrete, plants throughout, and a terrarium. The restaurant occasionally has live music and takes walk-ins.

The menu covers Sichuan and Shanghai-style dishes. Standouts include mapo tofu, a pickled fish stew, tea-smoked duck, and dim sum. The cold preserved egg with tofu in chili oil is one of the more distinct dishes, with silky tofu balancing the chewiness of the century egg in a spicy and savory sauce. The fish in chili oil takes a slightly different approach than most versions of the dish, with a tanginess that sets it apart. Cold sesame noodles and wok-fried cabbage are lighter options that work well alongside the heavier plates. The cocktail list includes drinks made with rum, shochu, and fresh fruit juices.

Source: Antidote NY
