Perfect For

New York City has enough restaurants to suit nearly any occasion, but the range of options also makes it easy to choose somewhere that does not quite fit the moment. A reservation at a small tasting menu restaurant does not work for a last-minute group of eight, and a loud barbecue spot is not the right setting for a quiet dinner with one person. Understanding what a meal actually needs before looking at specific restaurants narrows the options considerably and tends to lead to a better outcome.

Lucky Charlie

Lucky Charlie is a pizza and Italian-American restaurant on Irving Avenue in Bushwick, opened by Nino Coniglio, who also co-owns the Williamsburg Pizza group. The kitchen is built around what is claimed to be New York City's oldest operational coal oven, constructed by German bakers in the late 1800s and last used by Verde Coal Oven Pizza before that restaurant closed in 2015. The coal oven produces a distinctive char that extends through the crust, and the pies fall somewhere between New York and New Haven in style. The restaurant is open Wednesday through Sunday until 4am, making it one of the more reliable late-night options for full sit-down pizza in the city.

The menu offers three pizza formats: the classic with fior di latte and San Marzano tomato sauce, a red pie with sauce, garlic, and pecorino, and a white pie with stracciatella and asiago. Twelve toppings are available including fennel sausage, Sicilian anchovies, and Calabrian chili. The full food menu runs until 10pm and includes a seafood appetizer set of oysters Rockefeller, clams casino, shrimp oreganata, and grilled octopus, a ricotta and fennel sausage cannelloni, a Sicilian anelletti pasta baked with beef ragu and eggplant, and a tiramisu. Cocktails start at $11 and are served until 4am. The restaurant does not have a phone, so reservations and inquiries require an in-person visit. Weekend waits can run up to two hours.

Source: Brooklyn Magazine

Shawarma Bay

Shawarma Bay is a halal food truck on Sixth Avenue near 52nd Street in Midtown Manhattan, run by an Afghan team. The truck opens at 7:30pm and runs until 3am on weekdays and 4am on Fridays and Saturdays, making it one of the more accessible late-night options in Midtown. It sits a short walk from the better-known Adel's cart on the same avenue.

The menu centers on platters and wraps priced between $12 and $15. The chapli kebab is the signature item, a minced meat preparation seasoned with cumin and herbs that is less common at halal carts in the city than the standard gyro. The chicken shawarma and beef doner are the other main options. Each platter comes with long-grain rice, fries, and a small salad. Five sauces are available: white sauce, tzatziki, hot sauce, BBQ sauce, and lemon herb. The lemon herb in particular comes up frequently as a standout. Dessert options include baklava, vanilla custard, and rice pudding.

The truck takes card with a small surcharge. Staff take orders from the line before customers reach the window, which helps move the queue faster than comparable carts in the area. Wait times vary but tend to run shorter than at Adel's nearby, particularly on weeknights.

Source: Corner

Lillo Cucina

Lillo Cucina is a Roman Italian restaurant on Henry Street in Cobble Hill, Brooklyn, that has been operating since 2017. Chef-owner Giampietro Remia, known as Lillo, runs the entire restaurant by himself, handling orders, cooking, and service. The dining room has six tables and 17 seats total. The restaurant is cash only, does not serve alcohol, has no bathroom, and does not take reservations. Most dishes are priced around $15, with nothing on the menu exceeding $25, which is well below the typical cost of pasta at comparable restaurants in the city. The kitchen closes at 8:30pm.

The menu draws from Roman cooking traditions, with a printed menu supplemented by handwritten specials on pieces of paper posted to the wall. The rigatoni alla gricia, built around guanciale and parmesan, is one of the dishes the restaurant is best known for. The beet gnocchi and cacio e pepe are also strong options from the pasta section. The carciofi, artichokes prepared in olive oil, works well as a starter with bread. Pork ribs, branzino, and a selection of sandwiches named after Italian films are among the other options. The tiramisu is prepared in house and can be ordered to go without committing to a full meal.

The space fills up fast. Arriving early, around 4:30 to 5pm on weekdays, gives the best chance of getting a table without waiting.

Source: The Infatuation

Yi Ji Shi Mo Noodle Corp

Yi Ji Shi Mo Noodle Corp is a Cantonese takeout counter on Elizabeth Street in Chinatown that focuses entirely on cheung fun, a style of steamed rice roll common in Cantonese cooking. The kitchen staff grinds fresh rice into flour each morning using a stone mill and prepares each order on demand rather than in batches. The result is a wrapper considerably thinner and more delicate than versions made with pre-processed flour. The shop has no seating, is cash only, and opened in 2019. Nothing on the menu costs more than $10.

The menu is short. The Shrimp Party roll combines fresh shrimp, dried shrimp, cilantro, and egg. The Signature version incorporates roast pork alongside those ingredients. The rolls are at their best eaten directly outside after ordering, as the texture deteriorates once they sit.

The counter opens in the morning and draws a consistent crowd early in the day. Arriving outside of peak lunch hours reduces wait times considerably, which on busy days can stretch past 30 minutes.

Source: The Infatuation

Family Meal at Blue Hill

Family Meal at Blue Hill is a one-Michelin-star American restaurant on Washington Place in Greenwich Village, operating out of the original Blue Hill space that chef Dan Barber opened in 2000. The current format began as a short-term pop-up in 2021 as the restaurant reopened after the pandemic and became a permanent concept when the team found it more compelling than reverting to the previous approach. Chef de cuisine Mark Ordaz, who came up through Daniel Boulud's kitchens and spent several years at Momofuku, leads the kitchen day to day.

There is no printed menu. The kitchen composes a changing set of shared dishes based on what is arriving from partner farms, including the Barber family's farm in Massachusetts and the Stone Barns Center for Agriculture in New York. The meal moves through several rounds, from snacks and bread to appetizers and larger dishes, with the full selection placed at the table for everyone to share. The menu can shift daily depending on what ingredients are at their peak, which means no two visits are identical. A wine pairing is available. The price per person is $145 before tax and tip.

The restaurant is open Sunday through Thursday from 5:30pm to 9pm and Friday and Saturday until 9:30pm. Reservations are booked through Resy.

Source: The New York Times

Nubiani

Nubiani is a Korean barbecue restaurant on Fifth Avenue in Koreatown, reached by elevator to the third floor of a building on the corner of 32nd Street. The dining room is long and narrow with dim lighting, and seating is split between standard tables and a U-shaped bar where servers grill at the counter directly in front of diners. The bar setup allows for more attentive grilling throughout the meal. The restaurant is open until 11pm on weekdays and until 1am on Fridays and Saturdays.

The meal opens with a wide spread of banchan and a steamed egg soufflé. Meat can be ordered through set packages or individually. The beef package includes boneless short ribs, skirt steak, rib finger, and seasoned short ribs. The pork package covers pork belly, pork jowl, and seasoned shoulder. Each package includes a choice between kimchi stew and soybean paste stew. The marinated short rib is among the dishes that draw the most consistent attention. The spicy cold buckwheat noodles hold up well alongside the grilled meat and are worth adding to the order. Pricing is on the higher end for the neighborhood, with the small beef package starting at $150. A cash discount of 10 percent off the subtotal is available.

Reservations are accepted and are advisable for weekend evenings. The bar seats smaller parties and tends to book up ahead of standard table seating.

Source: The Infatuation

Late Night Food

A late night restaurant is one that stays open past midnight with a full kitchen still running. The focus is on food, not just the late hours. These are places worth going to after 10pm when most of the city has already stopped serving.

What to Expect

  • Kitchen open past midnight on most nights
  • Walk-in friendly with no reservation required
  • Full menu or close to it available late
  • Expect a livelier atmosphere than a typical dinner service
  • Some spots have long waits on weekends even late into the night

Cheap Eats

A cheap eat is a meal under $20 where the low cost comes from efficiency and a limited menu, not from cuts to ingredients or preparation. The price reflects how the restaurant operates, not the quality of what is served.

What to Expect

  • Most dishes under $20, often well under
  • Cash only is common at the best spots
  • Minimal seating or no seating at all
  • No reservations and sometimes a line
  • Speed and focus over atmosphere

Group Dinner

A group dinner restaurant needs to handle multiple people comfortably. That means enough table space, a menu that works across different preferences, and a format that allows conversation. Shared plates and set menus tend to work better for groups than individual ordering at busy restaurants.

What to Expect

  • Reservations are usually required and sometimes essential
  • Shared plates or a set menu format works best
  • Larger parties often pay a set gratuity automatically
  • Book well in advance for groups of six or more
  • Loud rooms are common at group-friendly restaurants
